About

I am a third year biomedical engineering student at the University of Guelph with a strong interest in the crossover between neuroscience and engineering. Through my coursework and hands on research, I have gained experience working with EMG systems, optical motion capture, force plates, and biomechanical modelling software.

What really drives me is the potential of neurotechnology to change how we understand and interact with the human nervous system. Whether that is decoding motor intent for prosthetic control or building brain inspired computing architectures, I want to be at the forefront of this field.

Independent Study

Effect of Load and Repetition Range on Biceps Muscle Activation

Compared normalized EMG activation in the long and short heads of the biceps brachii and brachioradialis across a control condition at 50% of 1RM and a high load condition at 75% of 1RM. The results showed approximately an 89% increase in BB short head activation under the heavier load along with measurable shoulder form deterioration indicated by a 3.4 degree kinematic drift throughout the set.

EMG Analysis

Measuring Muscle Activation During Isometric and Dynamic Elbow Contractions

Investigated how BB and BR EMG respond to increasing isometric joint moment, varying elbow angles at a constant load, and dynamic bicep curls. The study confirmed a nonlinear EMG to moment relationship along with angle dependent activation patterns across both muscles.

Motion Capture

Joint Angle Measurement During Submaximal and Maximal Broad Jumping

Assessed hip, knee, and ankle ROM and angular velocity during single leg and double leg broad jumps across 25% to 100% effort levels. The knee exhibited the largest sagittal ROM at 140.22 degrees and the highest peak angular velocity at 1702.5 degrees per second.

Force Analysis

Ground Reaction Force During Vertical Countermovement Jump

Analyzed vertical GRF asymmetry and impulse during countermovement jump take off and landing using dual force plates (Kistler piezoelectric and AMTI electromechanical) at 2000 Hz and 30 Hz sampling rates. The results confirmed dominant leg asymmetry and demonstrated how sampling rate is critical for capturing impact loading detail through FFT analysis.

In Progress

Optimizing Reservoir Computing Topologies for Neuromorphic Hardware Implementation

This study is focused on identifying the most efficient reservoir computing topology for neuromorphic chip design. The research is comparing three distinct reservoir network topologies: random, small world, and uncoupled architectures. Each topology is being evaluated in terms of computational efficiency, information capacity, signal to noise ratio, and how well it scales onto physical hardware. The project is also implementing a spiking neural network (SNN) architecture provided by the University of Texas to benchmark how biologically plausible network dynamics translate onto neuromorphic silicon. The end goal is to figure out which topology gives the best balance between information throughput, power consumption, and chip area so that future neuromorphic processors can be designed with an optimal reservoir structure from the ground up.
